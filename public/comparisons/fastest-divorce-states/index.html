Fastest Divorce States in the U.S.

Information Verified: January 2026 Sources: Official State Court Websites Content Type: Informational Resource (Not Legal Advice)

Divorce timelines vary significantly across the United States. While some states impose mandatory waiting periods of six months or longer, others allow divorces to be finalized in as little as a few weeks. The speed of the process depends on several factors including mandatory waiting periods, residency requirements, court processing times, and whether the divorce is contested or uncontested.

What Determines How Fast a Divorce Can Be Finalized?

Several factors influence how quickly a divorce can be completed in any given state:

  • Mandatory waiting periods — Many states require a set number of days between filing and finalization. This is the single biggest factor in determining the minimum possible timeline.
  • Residency requirements — Before filing, you must meet the state's residency requirement. Some states require as little as six weeks of residency, while others require six months or more.
  • Court scheduling — Even in states without waiting periods, courts must schedule hearings based on their caseload, which can add days or weeks to the timeline.
  • Whether children are involved — Divorces involving minor children often require additional time for custody arrangements, parenting plans, and in some states a longer mandatory waiting period.

States With Longer Divorce Waiting Periods

On the other end of the spectrum, several states impose significantly longer waiting periods or separation requirements before a divorce can be finalized. These extended timelines are intended to provide a cooling-off period and encourage reconciliation.

California 6 months Mandatory six-month waiting period from date respondent is served
Illinois 6 months separation Parties must live separate and apart for six months; may be waived by agreement

Texas 60 days Mandatory 60-day waiting period from date petition is filed
Vermont 6 months At least six months must pass from date defendant is served before final hearing
Virginia 6 months to 1 year Six months with no minor children and a separation agreement; one year if minor children are involved or no agreement

How Waiting Periods Affect Divorce Timelines

It is important to understand that even states with no mandatory waiting period still require time to process a divorce. Court clerks must review paperwork, judges must sign final decrees, and hearings must be scheduled. In practice, even the fastest uncontested divorces typically take several weeks to complete.

Additionally, the timelines above represent best-case scenarios for uncontested divorces where both parties agree on all terms. Contested divorces — those involving disputes over property division, child custody, or support — can take significantly longer regardless of the state's mandatory waiting period.

If you are considering filing for divorce and want to minimize the timeline, filing in a state where you already meet the residency requirement and where the waiting period is shortest will generally lead to the fastest resolution.
