Divorce Filing Requirements Vary by State

Each state sets its own residency rules for divorce eligibility. Some states require as little as six weeks of residency, while others require six months or more. Filing in the wrong jurisdiction or before meeting residency requirements can result in your case being dismissed.

Mandatory waiting periods also differ significantly. Certain states allow divorce finalization within weeks of filing, while others impose waiting periods of 60 days, 90 days, or longer before a court will issue a final decree.

Court filing fees range from under $100 to over $400 depending on the state and county. Many courts offer fee waiver programs for individuals who meet financial eligibility criteria. InFormer kits include the official fee waiver request forms for every state.

Understanding Divorce Filing in the United States

No-Fault Divorce Laws

All 50 states now allow some form of no-fault divorce, meaning a spouse can file without proving wrongdoing by the other party. The most commonly cited ground is "irreconcilable differences" or "irretrievable breakdown of the marriage." For example, California divorce forms are built around no-fault grounds exclusively, while states like New York also retain certain fault-based options.

Some states also retain fault-based grounds such as adultery, abandonment, or cruelty. Whether fault-based grounds affect property division or custody outcomes depends on the jurisdiction. State-specific details are available on each state's filing requirements page.

Contested vs. Uncontested Divorce

An uncontested divorce occurs when both spouses agree on all major issues, including property division, child custody, and support arrangements. These cases typically involve less paperwork, lower costs, and shorter timelines than contested proceedings.

InFormer divorce form kits are designed for uncontested cases. Individuals facing disagreements over assets, custody, or support may need to consult with a family law attorney to address those issues.

Financial Disclosure Requirements

Most states require both parties to complete financial disclosure forms as part of the divorce process. These documents typically include income statements, asset inventories, debt schedules, and monthly expense summaries. In Texas, for instance, a sworn inventory and appraisement is commonly required alongside the petition.

The specific forms and deadlines for financial disclosures vary by state. Failing to submit required disclosures can delay proceedings or result in court sanctions. Each InFormer kit identifies the disclosure obligations applicable to that state.

Parenting Plan Considerations

When minor children are involved, most courts require a parenting plan that outlines custody arrangements, visitation schedules, and decision-making responsibilities. Some states also mandate completion of a parenting education course before the divorce can be finalized — Florida divorce filings involving children, for example, require an approved parenting course.

InFormer offers separate kit options for cases with and without children. The "With Children" kit includes the additional forms and documentation typically required when minor children are part of the case.

Filing Timeline Expectations

The time between filing and finalization varies widely. Some states have no mandatory waiting period, while others require 30 to 90 days or more before a court will enter a final decree. Illinois divorce filing requirements include a mandatory waiting period, while Nevada is known for one of the shortest processing timelines in the country.

In general, uncontested cases with properly completed paperwork move through the system more quickly than contested or incomplete filings. Accurate, state-specific documentation can help reduce avoidable delays.
