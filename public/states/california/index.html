County-Specific Filing Considerations in California

California divorce petitions are filed with the Superior Court in the county where either spouse resides. While statewide rules govern the basic process, individual counties may have local procedural requirements, supplemental forms, or scheduling differences.

Los Angeles County

Los Angeles County Superior Court is one of the largest court systems in the country and handles a high volume of family law cases. Filers should be aware that Los Angeles County may require additional local forms alongside the standard Judicial Council forms. Court processing times can vary depending on the courthouse location within the county. The Stanley Mosk Courthouse in downtown Los Angeles is the primary family law filing location, though several district courthouses also accept filings. Self-help centers are available at multiple locations to assist self-represented litigants.

San Diego County

San Diego County Superior Court processes divorce filings through its Family Law Division. San Diego County provides a family law facilitator office that offers free assistance to self-represented parties with completing and filing divorce paperwork. The county may have specific local rules regarding case management conferences and mandatory disclosure timelines that supplement statewide requirements.

Orange County

Orange County Superior Court handles family law matters at the Lamoreaux Justice Center in Orange. The county offers a self-help center that assists individuals with form selection and procedural questions. Orange County follows standard Judicial Council forms but may have local rules regarding hearing scheduling, document formatting, and mandatory settlement conferences in contested matters.

Step-by-Step Divorce Process in California

The following outlines the general process for filing an uncontested divorce in California. Individual circumstances may affect the specific steps required.

  1. Confirm residency eligibility. At least one spouse must have resided in California for six months and in the filing county for three months.
  2. Complete the Petition (FL-100) and Summons (FL-110). The petition initiates the divorce case and identifies the relief being requested. The summons notifies the other party and imposes automatic temporary restraining orders on both spouses regarding property and children.
  3. File the petition with the Superior Court. Submit the completed petition, summons, and any required local forms to the clerk of the Superior Court in your county. Pay the filing fee or submit a fee waiver request (Form FW-001).
  4. Serve the respondent. California law requires that the other spouse be formally served with copies of the filed petition and summons. Service must be performed by someone other than the petitioner who is at least 18 years old. A Proof of Service (FL-115) must then be filed with the court.
  5. Complete preliminary financial disclosures. Both parties are required to exchange a Declaration of Disclosure (FL-140), an Income and Expense Declaration (FL-150), and a Schedule of Assets and Debts (FL-142). These disclosures are mandatory even in uncontested cases.
  6. Wait for the mandatory six-month period. California imposes a minimum six-month waiting period from the date the respondent is served. No final judgment can be entered before this period expires.
  7. Prepare the judgment documents. Once the waiting period has elapsed and all disclosures are complete, prepare the Judgment (FL-180) and any applicable attachments, including a marital settlement agreement if applicable.
  8. Submit the judgment to the court. File the completed judgment package with the court clerk for review. If the paperwork is in order, the judge will sign the judgment and the divorce becomes final.

This is a general overview for informational purposes. Specific requirements may vary by county and individual circumstances. Individuals with contested issues should consult a licensed attorney.

Frequently Asked Questions About Divorce in California

What are the residency requirements for divorce in California?

To file for divorce in California, you must meet specific residency criteria. At least one spouse must have lived in California for six months and in the county of filing for at least three months before filing for divorce.

How long does a divorce take in California?

The timeline for finalizing a divorce in California depends on mandatory waiting periods and court processing. California requires a mandatory six-month waiting period from the date the respondent is served before a divorce can be finalized.

How much does it cost to file for divorce in California?

Filing for divorce in California involves court fees that vary by county. Typical filing fees range from $435, though additional local court fees may apply depending on the county.

Can divorce filing fees be waived in California?

Individuals who cannot afford court fees in California may be eligible for a fee waiver. California courts allow eligible individuals to request a fee waiver based on income and financial circumstances using official Judicial Council forms.

What financial disclosures are required in a California divorce?

Both parties must exchange preliminary declarations of disclosure, including a Declaration of Disclosure (FL-140), an Income and Expense Declaration (FL-150), and a Schedule of Assets and Debts (FL-142). These disclosures are mandatory even when the divorce is uncontested and both spouses agree on all terms.

Is California a community property state for divorce?

Yes. California is one of nine community property states. Under California Family Code, most assets and debts acquired during the marriage are considered community property and are generally divided equally between the spouses upon divorce. Separate property, such as assets owned before the marriage or received as gifts or inheritance, is typically not subject to division.

What is a summary dissolution in California?

California offers a simplified divorce process called summary dissolution for couples who meet specific criteria, including a marriage of five years or less, no minor children, limited property and debts, and mutual agreement on all terms. Summary dissolution uses a joint petition (FL-800) and does not require formal service of process.

Are automatic temporary restraining orders issued when a California divorce is filed?

Yes. When a divorce petition is filed in California, automatic temporary restraining orders (ATROs) take effect for both spouses. These orders, printed on the back of the summons (FL-110), prohibit either party from transferring or hiding assets, changing insurance beneficiaries, or removing minor children from the state without written consent or a court order.
