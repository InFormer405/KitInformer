How to File for Divorce in Florida

The following outlines the general process for filing an uncontested dissolution of marriage in Florida. Individual circumstances, county procedures, or contested issues may affect the specific steps required.

  1. Confirm residency eligibility. At least one spouse must have been a resident of Florida for at least six months before filing. Residency can be established through a valid Florida driver's license, voter registration, or other documentation.
  2. Complete the Petition for Dissolution of Marriage. Florida uses standardized Supreme Court Approved Family Law Forms. For cases without children, use Form 12.901(b)(1). For cases with minor children, use Form 12.901(b)(2). The petition identifies both parties, states the grounds for dissolution (irretrievably broken marriage), and outlines the relief requested.
  3. File the petition with the Clerk of the Circuit Court. Submit the completed petition and any required attachments to the Clerk of the Circuit Court in the county where either spouse resides. Pay the filing fee (typically $400 to $410) or submit an Application for Determination of Civil Indigent Status if eligible for a fee waiver.
  4. Serve the other spouse. Florida law requires formal service of process on the non-filing spouse. Service may be completed by a sheriff, certified process server, or through certified mail in some circumstances. If the respondent agrees, they may file a Notice of Appearance or Waiver acknowledging receipt.
  5. Complete mandatory financial disclosures. Both parties must file a Financial Affidavit (Form 12.902(b) or (c) depending on income level) disclosing all income, expenses, assets, and liabilities. These disclosures are required even in uncontested cases.
  6. Complete the parenting course (if children are involved). Florida requires both parents to complete an approved Parent Education and Family Stabilization Course when minor children are part of the case. The course must be completed before the final hearing.
  7. Wait for the mandatory 20-day period. Florida imposes a minimum 20-day waiting period from the date the petition is filed before a final hearing can be scheduled. Courts may waive this period in certain circumstances.
  8. Attend the final hearing. In an uncontested dissolution, the petitioner appears before the judge for a brief final hearing. The judge reviews the settlement agreement, confirms both parties consent to the terms, and signs the Final Judgment of Dissolution of Marriage. The divorce is final once the judge signs the judgment.

This is a general overview for informational purposes. Specific requirements may vary by county and individual circumstances. Individuals with contested issues should consult a licensed attorney.

Frequently Asked Questions About Divorce in Florida

What are the residency requirements for divorce in Florida?

Before initiating divorce proceedings in Florida, residency standards must be met. At least one spouse must have been a resident of Florida for six months before filing for dissolution of marriage.

How long does a divorce take in Florida?

After filing, Florida imposes a required waiting period before the court can issue a final divorce decree. Florida requires a mandatory 20-day waiting period from the date the petition is filed before a final hearing can be held. Courts may waive this period in certain circumstances.

How much does it cost to file for divorce in Florida?

The cost of filing for divorce in Florida includes mandatory court fees. Typical filing fees range from $400 to $410, though additional fees may apply depending on the county and type of filing.

Can divorce filing fees be waived in Florida?

If filing fees present a financial hardship, Florida courts may grant a waiver. Florida courts allow individuals who meet financial eligibility requirements to apply for a determination of civil indigent status to waive filing fees.
