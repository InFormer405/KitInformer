How to File for Divorce in Illinois

The following outlines the general process for filing an uncontested dissolution of marriage in Illinois. Individual circumstances, county procedures, or contested issues may affect the specific steps required.

  1. Confirm residency eligibility. At least one spouse must have been a resident of Illinois for at least 90 days before filing. The petition is filed in the circuit court of the county where either spouse resides.
  2. Prepare the Petition for Dissolution of Marriage. The petition is the document that formally initiates the case. It identifies both parties, states the grounds for dissolution (irreconcilable differences, the sole ground in Illinois), and outlines the relief requested regarding property division, support, and, if applicable, child custody and parenting time.
  3. File the petition with the Circuit Clerk. Submit the completed petition and any required attachments to the Circuit Clerk's office in the appropriate county. Pay the filing fee (typically $250 to $388 depending on the county) or submit an Application for Waiver of Court Fees if eligible.
  4. Serve the respondent. Illinois requires that the non-filing spouse receive formal notice of the proceedings. Service can be completed by a sheriff, special process server, or by publication in certain circumstances. In uncontested cases, the respondent may file an Entry of Appearance and Waiver of Service, acknowledging the case without formal service.
  5. Satisfy the separation requirement. Illinois requires the parties to have lived separate and apart for a continuous period of six months before the court may enter a judgment of dissolution. However, this requirement can be waived if both parties agree in writing, which is common in uncontested cases.
  6. Complete financial disclosures. Both parties must exchange financial information including income, assets, debts, and expenses. Illinois requires a Financial Affidavit or disclosure statement. In cases involving children, both parties must also complete a Child Support Worksheet.
  7. Complete the parenting education program (if children are involved). Illinois requires parents to complete a court-approved parenting education program when minor children are part of the case, unless the court waives this requirement. A parenting plan or allocation judgment addressing custody (allocation of parental responsibilities) and parenting time must also be prepared.
  8. Attend the prove-up hearing. In an uncontested dissolution, the petitioner appears before the judge for a brief prove-up hearing. The judge reviews the settlement agreement, confirms both parties consent to the terms, and enters the Judgment of Dissolution of Marriage. The divorce is final once the judge signs the judgment.

This is a general overview for informational purposes. Specific requirements may vary by county and individual circumstances. Individuals with contested issues should consult a licensed attorney.
