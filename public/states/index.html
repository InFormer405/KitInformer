Divorce laws vary significantly across all 50 states. Each state sets its own residency requirements, waiting periods, filing fees, and filing procedures. Explore the filing requirements, residency rules, waiting periods, and typical filing fees for your state below.

Why Divorce Rules Vary by State

The United States does not have a single federal divorce law. Instead, each state has its own family law system that governs how divorces are filed, processed, and finalized. This means:

  • Residency Requirements: States set minimum residency periods (typically 6 months to 1 year) before you can file for divorce in that state.
  • Waiting Periods: After filing, most states impose mandatory waiting periods ranging from 0 to 6 months before a divorce can be finalized.
  • Filing Fees: Court filing fees vary significantly by state and county, ranging from under $200 to over $500.
  • Fault vs. No-Fault: Some states allow only no-fault divorce, while others offer both fault and no-fault options.
  • Fee Waivers: Many states offer fee waiver programs for individuals who cannot afford filing fees.
