New York Residency Requirement

At least one spouse must have been a resident of New York for a continuous period of at least two years before filing, or one year if the marriage took place in New York or the grounds for divorce arose in New York.

New York Fault Type

New York allows both no-fault and fault-based divorce. The no-fault ground is an irretrievable breakdown of the marriage for at least six months.

Parenting Classes

New York does not have a statewide mandate for parenting classes, but individual courts or judges may order parenting education programs when children are involved.

How to File for Divorce in New York

The following outlines the general process for filing an uncontested divorce in New York. Individual circumstances, county procedures, or contested issues may affect the specific steps required.

  1. Confirm residency eligibility. New York has multiple residency paths. If both spouses are New York residents at the time of filing, a one-year continuous residency is required. If the marriage took place in New York or the grounds for divorce arose in New York, one year of continuous residency by either spouse is sufficient. Otherwise, at least one spouse must have been a continuous resident for two years before filing.
  2. Prepare the Summons and Complaint or Summons with Notice. The Summons with Notice is the most common method for initiating an uncontested divorce. It identifies the parties, states the grounds for divorce (typically irretrievable breakdown for at least six months under DRL §170(7)), and specifies the relief sought. Alternatively, a Verified Complaint may be filed with additional detail.
  3. Purchase an Index Number from the County Clerk. Before filing, you must purchase an Index Number from the County Clerk's office in the county where the action will be filed. The filing fee is $210. A separate $125 Request for Judicial Intervention (RJI) fee is required when the case is ready for court review.
  4. Serve the defendant. New York requires personal service of the Summons with Notice or Summons and Complaint on the non-filing spouse. Service must be performed by a person who is not a party to the action and who is at least 18 years old. An Affidavit of Service must then be filed with the court as proof.
  5. Wait for the defendant's response. After service, the defendant has 20 days to respond if served in person within New York, or 30 days if served by another method. In an uncontested case, the defendant typically signs an Affidavit of Defendant acknowledging receipt and waiving the right to answer.
  6. Complete financial disclosures. Both parties must file a Statement of Net Worth listing income, expenses, assets, and liabilities. In uncontested cases, a Sworn Statement of Removal of Barriers to Remarriage (if applicable) and other required affidavits must also be prepared.
  7. Prepare the uncontested divorce packet. Compile the full set of documents including the Note of Issue, Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law, Judgment of Divorce, Part 130 Certification, and a separation or settlement agreement signed by both parties and notarized. If children are involved, include the Child Support Worksheet and related custody provisions.
  8. Submit the packet to the court for review. File the completed uncontested divorce packet with the County Clerk along with the RJI fee. A judge reviews the documents without a hearing in most uncontested cases. If the paperwork is in order, the judge signs the Judgment of Divorce and the divorce becomes final.

This is a general overview for informational purposes. Specific requirements may vary by county and individual circumstances. Individuals with contested issues should consult a licensed attorney.

Frequently Asked Questions About Divorce in New York

What are the residency requirements for divorce in New York?

Residency eligibility is a prerequisite for filing divorce paperwork in New York. At least one spouse must have been a resident of New York for a continuous period of at least two years before filing, or one year if the marriage took place in New York or the grounds for divorce arose in New York.

How long does a divorce take in New York?

How quickly a divorce is finalized in New York is governed by statutory waiting requirements. New York does not impose a mandatory waiting period after filing for an uncontested divorce. However, contested cases may take longer depending on court scheduling.

How much does it cost to file for divorce in New York?

New York courts charge filing fees when a divorce petition is submitted. The filing fee for a divorce action in New York is $210, plus an additional $125 Request for Judicial Intervention fee. Additional county surcharges may apply.

Can divorce filing fees be waived in New York?

Fee waiver programs are available in New York for eligible individuals filing for divorce. New York courts allow individuals to apply for a fee waiver (poor person relief) if they cannot afford the filing costs, using a formal application submitted to the court.
