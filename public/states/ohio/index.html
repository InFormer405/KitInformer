Ohio Residency Requirement

At least one spouse must have been a resident of Ohio for six months and a resident of the county where filing for at least 90 days before filing for divorce.

Ohio Divorce Waiting Period

Ohio does not have a mandatory waiting period for divorce actions, but dissolution (uncontested) cases require a waiting period of 30 to 90 days from filing before a final hearing.

Ohio Divorce Filing Fees

Filing fees vary by county, typically ranging from $200 to $350 depending on the type of filing and county.

Ohio Filing Fee Waiver

Ohio courts allow individuals who meet financial hardship criteria to file an Affidavit of Indigency to request a waiver of court filing fees.

Ohio Fault Type

Ohio allows both no-fault and fault-based divorce. No-fault grounds include incompatibility and living separate and apart for one year without cohabitation.

Parenting Classes

Ohio does not have a statewide parenting class mandate, but many counties require parents to complete a parenting education program when minor children are involved.

How to File for Divorce in Ohio

The following outlines the general process for filing an uncontested dissolution of marriage in Ohio. Ohio distinguishes between "divorce" (where one party files against the other) and "dissolution" (where both parties file jointly by agreement). This guide covers the dissolution process, which is the most common path for uncontested cases. Individual circumstances or county procedures may affect the specific steps required.

  1. Confirm residency eligibility. At least one spouse must have been a resident of Ohio for at least six months and a resident of the county where the petition will be filed for at least 90 days before filing.
  2. Reach agreement on all terms. In a dissolution, both parties must agree on all issues before filing, including property division, spousal support, and, if applicable, child custody (allocation of parental rights and responsibilities), parenting time, and child support. These terms are documented in a Separation Agreement signed by both spouses.
  3. Prepare the Petition for Dissolution of Marriage. The petition is filed jointly by both spouses. It includes the Separation Agreement and identifies both parties, the date and place of marriage, the names and birth dates of any minor children, and a statement that both parties desire dissolution.
  4. File the petition with the Clerk of Courts. Submit the completed petition, Separation Agreement, and any required local forms to the Clerk of Courts in the Domestic Relations Division of the Court of Common Pleas. Pay the filing fee (typically $200 to $350 depending on the county) or file an Affidavit of Indigency if eligible for a fee waiver.
  5. Complete financial disclosures. Both parties must prepare and exchange financial disclosure statements listing all income, assets, debts, and expenses. Ohio courts require full financial transparency even in agreed cases. If children are involved, both parties must also complete a Child Support Computation Worksheet.
  6. Complete the parenting education program (if children are involved). While Ohio does not have a statewide mandate, many counties require parents to complete a court-approved parenting education program when minor children are part of the case. Check with the local Domestic Relations Division for county-specific requirements.
  7. Wait for the hearing window. Ohio requires a waiting period of 30 to 90 days from the date the petition is filed before a final hearing can be held. The court schedules the hearing within this window. Both parties must appear at the hearing.
  8. Attend the final hearing. Both spouses appear before the judge at the final hearing. The judge reviews the Separation Agreement, confirms both parties entered into it voluntarily, and verifies that the terms are fair and equitable. If the court approves, the judge signs the Decree of Dissolution and the marriage is officially terminated.

This is a general overview for informational purposes. Specific requirements may vary by county and individual circumstances. Individuals with contested issues should consult a licensed attorney.
