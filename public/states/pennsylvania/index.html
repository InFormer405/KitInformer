Pennsylvania Residency Requirement

At least one spouse must have been a bona fide resident of Pennsylvania for at least six months before filing for divorce.

Pennsylvania Divorce Waiting Period

Pennsylvania requires a 90-day waiting period from the date of service of the divorce complaint before the case can proceed on a no-fault basis with mutual consent. Without mutual consent, a one-year separation period is required.

Pennsylvania Filing Fee Waiver

Pennsylvania courts allow individuals who meet income guidelines to file a Petition to Proceed In Forma Pauperis (IFP) to waive court filing fees.

Pennsylvania Fault Type

Pennsylvania allows both no-fault and fault-based divorce. No-fault grounds include mutual consent (after 90 days) or a two-year separation.

Parenting Classes

Pennsylvania does not have a statewide parenting class requirement, but many counties require parents to attend a parenting education program when minor children are involved.

How to File for Divorce in Pennsylvania

The following outlines the general process for filing a no-fault uncontested divorce in Pennsylvania with mutual consent. Individual circumstances, county procedures, or contested issues may affect the specific steps required.

  1. Confirm residency eligibility. At least one spouse must have been a bona fide resident of Pennsylvania for at least six months before filing. The complaint is filed in the Court of Common Pleas in the county where either spouse resides.
  2. Prepare the Divorce Complaint. The complaint initiates the divorce action and identifies both parties, the date and place of marriage, the grounds for divorce, and the relief requested. For a no-fault divorce with mutual consent, the complaint is filed under Section 3301(c) of the Pennsylvania Divorce Code. If mutual consent is not available, a two-year separation under Section 3301(d) may be used instead.
  3. File the complaint with the Prothonotary. Submit the completed complaint and any required cover sheets to the Prothonotary's office in the appropriate county. Pay the filing fee (typically $200 to $350 depending on the county) or file a Petition to Proceed In Forma Pauperis if eligible for a fee waiver.
  4. Serve the defendant. Pennsylvania requires that the non-filing spouse be formally served with the divorce complaint. Service is typically handled by the sheriff's office or a competent adult who is not a party to the action. The defendant may also accept service voluntarily by signing an Acceptance of Service form.
  5. Wait for the mandatory 90-day period. Under Section 3301(c), a 90-day waiting period begins from the date the defendant is served with the divorce complaint. No affidavits of consent can be filed until this 90-day period has elapsed. This waiting period is a statutory requirement and cannot be shortened.
  6. File the Affidavits of Consent. After the 90-day waiting period, both spouses must sign and file separate Affidavits of Consent confirming that the marriage is irretrievably broken and that each party consents to the divorce. Both affidavits must be filed with the court before the divorce can proceed.
  7. Complete financial disclosures and property settlement. Both parties should prepare and exchange information about income, assets, debts, and expenses. A marital settlement agreement addressing property division, spousal support, and (if applicable) child custody, support, and visitation should be drafted and signed by both parties. If children are involved, many counties require completion of a parenting education program.
  8. File the Praecipe to Transmit Record and Divorce Decree. Once the affidavits and settlement agreement are filed, submit a Praecipe to Transmit Record to the court requesting entry of the divorce decree. A judge reviews the documents and, if everything is in order, signs the Divorce Decree. The divorce is final once the decree is entered by the court. No hearing is required in most uncontested mutual consent cases.

This is a general overview for informational purposes. Specific requirements may vary by county and individual circumstances. Individuals with contested issues should consult a licensed attorney.
