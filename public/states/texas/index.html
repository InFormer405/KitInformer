How to File for Divorce in Texas

The following outlines the general process for filing an uncontested divorce in Texas. Individual circumstances, county-specific rules, or contested issues may affect the steps required.

  1. Confirm residency eligibility. At least one spouse must have been a Texas resident for at least six months and a resident of the county where the petition will be filed for at least 90 days.
  2. Prepare the Original Petition for Divorce. The petition is the document that formally initiates the divorce case. It identifies both spouses, states the grounds for divorce (most commonly insupportability), and outlines the relief being requested, including property division and, if applicable, child custody arrangements.
  3. File the petition with the District Clerk. Submit the completed petition to the District Clerk's office in the county where either spouse resides. Pay the filing fee (typically $250 to $350) or file a Statement of Inability to Afford Payment of Court Costs if eligible for a fee waiver.
  4. Serve the respondent. Texas law requires the non-filing spouse to be formally notified of the divorce proceeding. Service can be accomplished through a process server, constable, or sheriff. Alternatively, the respondent may sign a Waiver of Service, which is common in uncontested cases where both parties agree.
  5. Wait for the mandatory 60-day cooling-off period. Texas requires a minimum 60-day waiting period from the date the petition is filed before a court can grant the divorce. No final decree can be entered during this period.
  6. Complete financial disclosures. Both parties should prepare a sworn inventory and appraisement listing all community and separate property, debts, and financial obligations. While not always required in agreed divorces, courts may request this documentation.
  7. Draft the Final Decree of Divorce. The decree is the court order that finalizes the divorce. It addresses property division, spousal support if applicable, and child custody and support arrangements if minor children are involved. Both parties should review and sign the decree before submission.
  8. Attend the prove-up hearing. In an uncontested divorce, the petitioner typically appears before the judge for a brief hearing to confirm the terms of the agreement. The judge reviews the decree, asks a series of standard questions, and signs the final order if everything is in order. The divorce is final once the judge signs the decree.

This is a general overview for informational purposes. Specific requirements may vary by county and individual circumstances. Individuals with contested issues should consult a licensed attorney.
